The central finding

Reactive, not anticipatory

For each major banking-policy event, the corpus compares scholarship in the three years before versus the three years after. Most topics surge only once the statute or crisis has already arrived — the literature follows policy more than it leads it.

All Reactive (lags) Anticipatory (leads)
How to read this: bars show article counts in the 3 years pre-event (grey) vs. post-event (blue). “Reactive” means the post-event window is meaningfully larger. Correlation isn't causation — co-movement can reflect shared drivers, and this measures topic volume, not influence.

About the data Research draft

This dashboard visualizes an original corpus of U.S. banking-law review articles (2000–2025) assembled from open sources — law-school institutional repositories (bepress/Digital Commons), open journal systems, publisher sitemaps, OpenAlex, and Crossref. Topics are assigned by a rule-based classifier over a 30+ category taxonomy.

Known limits, stated plainly: truly paywalled or restrictively-licensed sources (HeinOnline, Westlaw, Lexis, and the Banking Law Journal) were deliberately not scraped and remain undercounted. Earlier drafts flagged the Yale Law Journal and Stanford Law Review as gaps; a journal-of-record verification pass shows that was largely an attribution artifact — flagship pieces are captured through faculty repositories and re-attributed to where they actually appeared (e.g. Rauterberg & Zhang, Shadow Banking and Securities Law, 77 Stan. L. Rev. 563 (2025)). Yale is well covered; Stanford genuinely publishes little bank-regulation scholarship (its financial-law work is corporate/bankruptcy, outside this taxonomy).

Citations & venues: each article is now attributed to its journal of record — never SSRN or a repository — and carries a Bluebook citation; the ~13% whose published venue can't be recovered from open sources are flagged for manual completion rather than mislabeled. Congressional “presence” is a name-match (date-filtered to 2000–2026; namesake collisions flagged, e.g. Sunstein/Smith). Lead/lag measures topic volume around events, not causal influence. Figures are best read as directional.

Built by Jacob Gerszten (independent researcher) as a companion to his working papers on the OCC national trust charter, the GENIUS Act, and state-issued stablecoins. Charts are generated from local CSVs by build_data.py; no external services.
