The central finding

Reactive, not anticipatory

For each major banking-policy event, the corpus compares scholarship in the three years before versus the three years after. Most topics surge only once the statute or crisis has already arrived — the literature follows policy more than it leads it.

All Reactive (lags) Anticipatory (leads)
How to read this: bars show article counts in the 3 years pre-event (grey) vs. post-event (blue). “Reactive” means the post-event window is meaningfully larger. Correlation isn't causation — co-movement can reflect shared drivers, and this measures topic volume, not influence.

About the data Research draft

This dashboard visualizes an original corpus of U.S. banking-law review articles (2000–2025) assembled from open sources — law-school institutional repositories (bepress/Digital Commons), open journal systems, publisher sitemaps, OpenAlex, and Crossref. Topics are assigned by a rule-based classifier over a 30+ category taxonomy.

Known limits, stated plainly: sources behind paywalls or restrictive terms (HeinOnline, Westlaw, Lexis, and some flagship reviews) were deliberately not scraped, so venues such as the Yale Law Journal, Stanford Law Review, and the Banking Law Journal are undercounted. Congressional “presence” is a name-match and can collide with namesakes (flagged). Lead/lag measures topic volume around events, not causal influence. Figures are best read as directional.

Built by Jacob Gerszten (independent researcher) as a companion to his working papers on the OCC national trust charter, the GENIUS Act, and state-issued stablecoins. Charts are generated from local CSVs by build_data.py; no external services.
